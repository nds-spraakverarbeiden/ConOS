# Fragments and variants

C is the base version. None of the other manuscripts or the fragments provides any text not also found in C, but it also appears to have been written in England (traces of Old English).

since Behaghel, M is assumed to be most "correct" (Price 2010, p.3).

For L and P, entire pages are worn and difficult to read (Price 2010, p.4).

It seems the tokenization over any of that data is posthoc. On M, tokenization seems almost random. Over L and P, the digital edition by Price (2010) doesn't provide any tokenization. For processing this data, we need to develop a tokenizer, first.

For the manually annotated subset, it might make most sense to focus on passages attested in different manuscripts.

If we ever publish a corpus that contains multiple variants, we need a numbering scheme that makes redundancies visible:

- prefix "h." verse number . text, e.g., "h.5385.p" for line 5385 in Ms. p
- for incomplete verses, use a for first, b for second (or last) half verse
- for multi-line spans, use start-end
- a user can then identify matching overlapping spans and weigh the evidence accordingly

Word segmentation is a massive issue in any those texts, as are amendments in the electronic editions. Price (2010) is very close to the source, but gives two different segmentations on pages 69 and after and in the appendix. The former is more in line with conventional word boundaries, the appendix probably is from an early working version. Our L segmentation is Price's original, our P segmentation is from the join of text and appendix: whenever he inserted a word boundary in either, we preserve it.

The Titus editions of L and P are apparently amended according to BT, and also use BT word segmentation.
